# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

**Phase 1 (now):** Freelance clients. Engineering managers, manufacturing operations leads, and procurement decision-makers at precision machining shops, industrial OEMs, and pump/power equipment companies. They are evaluating whether Mark has the fabrication credibility and technical depth to handle their mechanical design, DFM review, or internal tooling work. They have seen dozens of generic CAD-jockey portfolios and are looking for proof that this person has actually touched the parts, run the machines, and survived the shop floor.

**Phase 2 (6+ months):** Long-term employment. Recruiters and hiring managers at mid-size manufacturers scanning for a mechanical designer who can also build internal software tools without needing a separate dev team. The portfolio needs to work for both audiences without feeling like a job-seeker's resume page.

## Product Purpose

A single-page portfolio site that functions as a credibility engine. The visitor should leave knowing three things: (1) Mark designs parts that survive fabrication, assembly, and field use because he has personally done all three, (2) his documentation and GD&T are built with inspection and tool life in mind, not just geometry, and (3) the software and AI work is operational proof, not a pivot away from manufacturing. Success is a qualified lead reaching the contact form.

## Brand Personality

Blunt, competent, floor-tested.

Not polished-corporate. Not startup-cute. Not "innovation-thought-leader." The voice of someone who says "sub-thou" because he has held the gage, not because he read it in a spec sheet. Technical specificity over marketing abstraction. Shop talk over buzzwords.

## Anti-references

- Generic Wix/Squarespace developer portfolios with gradient cards, glassmorphism, and "I'm passionate about building things" copy
- SaaS-cream landing pages: beige backgrounds, pill-shaped buttons, rounded-everything, generic icon grids, three-column feature cards
- Overly "techy-dark-mode-neon" developer portfolios that signal software-first identity and treat mechanical work as a footnote
- Any portfolio that leads with frameworks and languages instead of what was actually built and why it mattered
- Dribbble-bait: beautiful but says nothing, all motion and no substance

## Design Principles

1. **Show the work, not the wrapper.** Real project photos, real numbers, real outcomes. The design exists to frame fabrication credibility, not to be admired on its own.
2. **Specificity is the brand.** "Sub-thou tolerances" beats "precision engineering." "Sin blocks and Micro Height" beats "advanced inspection." Every generic phrase is a missed opportunity to prove depth.
3. **Manufacturing-first hierarchy.** Mechanical design and fabrication experience are the primary identity. Software and AI tools are supporting proof that the manufacturing knowledge scales. Never reverse this order.
4. **Earned confidence, not performed confidence.** The tone is direct because the experience is real. No hedging, no overselling. Let the testimonials and project outcomes carry the weight.
5. **Floor-ready, not portfolio-pretty.** The aesthetic should feel like it belongs in a precision shop's front office, not a design agency's Behance page. Technical, grounded, with just enough visual ambition to signal competence without signaling vanity.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA baseline. Ensure sufficient contrast on all text (especially over dark backgrounds and project imagery). Respect prefers-reduced-motion. No seizure-inducing animations. Alt text on all project images should describe what is shown with technical accuracy, not marketing fluff.
